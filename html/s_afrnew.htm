The New Republic was incorporated in Transvaal in 1888. Although the text is written in Afrikaans, the date is written in English. I've seen tete-beche, double prints, imperforate stamps and minor printing errors (letters missing, 'd' slanting in the pence values, perforation too narrow, etc.). It is doubtful any stamps above the value of 6 p were actually used for postal purposes.

These stamps were presumably handstamped. I've seen a sheet of 25 (5 x 5) 2 p stamps.

For the specialist: These stamps were issued on yellow paper or on grey paper with cloth threads (granite paper; similar to some of the stamps of Austria). Some stamps are embossed with a large seal of the republic. The stamps exist without any dates (1887-88) or with dates (1886-87).
The dates that can be found are: 9 JAN 86, 13 JAN 86, 20 JAN 86, 24 JAN 86, 10 FEB 86, 7 MAR 86, 17 MAR 86, 14 April 86, 14 May 86, 21 MAY 86, 24 MAY 86, 26 May 86, 30 Jun 86, JUL 7 86, 19 AUG 86, 30 AUG 86, 6 SEP 86, 13 Sep 86, 6 Oct 86, 13 OCT 86, 3 Nov 86, 24 NOV 86, - DEC 86, 2 DEC 86, 4 JAN 87, 17 JAN 87, 20 Jan 87 (source: my 1938 Senf catalogue).

Elliptic purple (fiscal?) cancel "VRYHEID" with date in the center. Also a fiscal(?) cancel in an ellipse with "NIEUWE REPUBLIEK VRIJHEID" and "A. de Vlettar" inside. Used stamps are actually not that common. Also two 3 p stamps with a "232" numeral cancel from Cape of Good Hope.

Postal stationary in the same design (2 p violet, 4 Jan 87) was issued in 1887.

Many forgeries exist (by the way, I'm not sure if all the above stamps are genuine), genuine stamps are always perforated 11 1/2; Genuine stamps have the upper part of the first "E" in "REPUBLIEK" with an 'accent':


('accent' above the "E")


Enlarged: Accent on first "E" of "REPUBLIEK" and oblique bottom right end of the last "E" of this word.

However, be careful, there is a forgery which has this 'accent'. The paper is incorrect on all the forgeries.  No one has been able to recreate this older type of "granite" paper that was used for printing tests (information thanks to Ron Carlson).


Fournier forgeries as they can be found in 'The Fournier Album of Philatelic Forgeries' (reduced sizes)

The forger Fournier advertises his 'stamps' in his 1914 pricelist: he lists as first choice forgeries:
1) the 1 and 4 d (5 values for 2.50 Swiss Francs);
2) 1 d embossed (3 values for 1.50 Swiss Francs) and
3) 1 and 4 d embossed (4 values for 2 Swiss Francs).
Furthermore he offers as second choice forgeries the 1 p (2 values) for 0.50 Swiss Francs. In 'The Fournier album of philatelic forgeries' pictures of his forgeries can be seen.
In my opinion, in the above 1 p forgeries, the line below the "d" is bit too far to the left.

The above stamp is a forgery, nicely done on granite paper, but the type faces are slightly wrong (information obtained thanks to Ron Carlson USA), it looks like a Fournier forgery to me as well.

The forger Oneglia offers the 1 p violet on yellow and the 1 p violet on grey in his 1906 catalogue for Fr.0.20 per piece. I have not yet been able to discover such a forgery.
