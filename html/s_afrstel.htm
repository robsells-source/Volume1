The republic of Stellaland existed from 1882 to 1885, and was then divided between Transvaal and British Bechuanaland. Stellaland did not cancel its stamps with the usual cancelling devices, instead only penstroked were used (mostly the initials of the postmaster "FH" and date). Cancelled stamps could have occasionally occured when the letters arrived in neighbouring countries (the town of Barkly in the Cape, but most are simply forgeries).

1884 Arms of Stellaland, inscription "REPUBLIEK STELLALAND POSTZEGEL"

  3 p orange (yellow)
  4 p green
  6 p violet
  1 Sh green

Surcharged

  "Twee" (red, in fancy letters) on 4 p green

For the specialist: these stamps have no watermark and are perforated 12 (also 11 1/2). They were issued on the 1st February 1884 and printed by Van de Sandt de Villiers in Cape Town and used until 2nd December 1885. The word "POSTZEGEL" means "stamp". The surcharged stamp was was not issued, the stock was sold to a dealer (The forged stamps of all countries by J.Dorn). Only 384 stamps were surcharged. The 1 p, 3 p, 4 p, and 1 Sh were issued in sheets of 96 stamps (twelve rows of 8), the 6 p was issued in sheets of 117 (13 rows of 9). There are 8 main types of the 1 p, 3 p, 4 p and 1 Sh and 9 types of the 6 p stamp. The remainders were confiscated by the British and sold to Whitfield King & Co in 1886. Stamps with one side imperforate exist (horizontal or vertical). Black 6 p proofs (perforate or imperforate) are known.

Some information was given to me by Kas Erné (The Netherlands), which I summarize as follows:
After the British victory in Stellaland, the British forces confiscated all Stellaland stamps and subsequently send them to London, where they ended up in the stamp trade. The stamps were printed by Van de Sandt de Villiers & Co in Capetown, after a first request to print the stamps to Hartley & Son (Kimberley) failed (Hartley received a image of the desired stamps, but he never replied). The stamps were used from 1 April 1884 to 2 December 1885. The surrounding countries (Transvaal, Orange Freestate and Cape of Good Hope) did not recognize the stamps and additional postage had to be paid for letters in transit. Cancelling devices did not exists and the postmaster applied his signature and a date on the stamps. The letters were then transferred to Christiana (Transvaal). Such genuine letters are very rare.
Forgeries were already reported in the philatelic press as early as 1887. One (unlikely) theory is that a fired employee of the printer (de Villiers) took a printing stone home and made forgeries of those. Another theory states that the printer himself (de Villiers) continued making reprints since Stellaland had not paid the bill (this does not explain the difference in design between the forgeries and the genuine stamps). It could also be possible that the printer Hartley made forgeries in order to compensate for the expenses that he had made (with a possibly slightly different design that he had received, possibly the most common forgery shown below could originate from this source).
The 'Twee' overprint is most likely an unauthorized issue created by the postmaster of Vrijburg.
Christiana in Transvaal used the numeral "6." cancel and can sometimes be found on stamps of Stellaland. The latest stamps were just cancelled by a (blue) penstroke.

Many forgeries exist (since the stamps have no watermark and a relatively easy design), the first forgeries already appeared in 1887. Examples of forgeries:


Forgeries with "VICTORIA", "A07" and also a New Zealand Dunedin cancel.

Distinguishing characteristics of these (classical) most common forgery type:
1) The left leaf in the bottom should not touch the ribbon in the genuine stamps.
2) There should be two shading lines entering the star in the shield from the left hand side (this is absent in the forgeries).
3) The perforation is also different from the genuine stamps (genuine stamps have 17 teeth at the top and bottom).

I have even seen forgeries of the 1 p stamp in the wrong colour: green instead of red. Forged cancels on genuine stamps exist. Also genuine stamps with the perforation cut off (to imitate imperforate proofs) exist.
