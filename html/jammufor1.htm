1866 Circular issues


1/2 anna (3 strokes in the center), 1 anna (vertical stroke with curved one below) and three 4 a (single stroke, which actually represents the number 1/4 for 1/4 Rupee), presumably genuine.


Genuine watercolor issues, images obtained from a Cherrystone auction. 1/2 a black and 1 a blue.

I have seen forgeries of these stamps, printed from the original die. They are different from the so-called Brighton forgeries made by Harold Treherne from 1902 to 1907. These forgeries are rather deceptive. See also http://www.sspak.com/library/India/Jammu%20&%20Kashmir%20Forgeries.pdf.
The site http://www.kashmirstamps.ca/SMcontents.html contains valuable information about these stamps. It is an (improved?) online version of the book 'The Stamps of Jammu-Kashmir' by Alexander Séfi & C.H. Mortimer (1937). Publisher: Séfi, Pemberton & Co., Ltd., 12 South Molton Street, W.1. London. 322 pp, 59 black & white plates. The forgeries described are the so-called 'Die I' forgeries (longtime believed to be the genuine stamps!) and 'Missing Die' forgeries.

 

Die I forgeries:

Once considered to be genuine and quite rare (and hence at some stage very expensive....). They are always printed in watercolor. The outer circles are not circular enough and the text is badly done (which was considered as a sign of genuineness, which forged the Jammu and Kasmir authorities to make a new die with better lettering). Only the 1/2 a and 4 a were forged (possibly in Paris France in the 1870's), they exist with red seal cancels:


Water color forgery. This might actually be a "Die I" forgery, once considered to be genuine.

They were considered to be genuine up to 1899, when Sir David Masson went to Kashmir and published an article in the Philatelic Journal of India, Vol.III, 1899 (source Dawson).

 

Missing Die forgeries:

'Missing Die' forgeries, believed to be from a 'missing die' that could not be found in the archive, it later turned out to be forgeries. These forgeries appear to have been made (and sold) by corrupt post office officials to stamp collectors, but also to replace genuine stamps. When the archives were checked for genuine stamps, most of them were replaced by these 'Missing Die' forgeries. They appear to have been made in the 1890's. There are two types of the 4 a forgeries.


'Missing die' forgery of the 1/2 a value, the three strokes in the center should not touch the white 'sun' in the genuine stamps. In the 'Missing Die' forgeries it does. See also https://www.stampboards.com/viewtopic.php?f=33&t=39738. The character on top that looks like a "3" extends its foot towards the left and touches the character to the left of it (in genuine stamps it bends away to the right).


Missing Die forgery of the 1/2 a with a forged Dak Jammu cancel. Also a forged cancel on a yellow and black Missing Die forgery.


Block of six Missing Die forgeries.


'Missing Die' forgery of the 1 a value. The upwards stroke of the central character points straight to the "3" (it points to the right of it in the genuine stamps). The inverted "R" shaped letter at the bottom left has a split leg.


'Missing Die' forgery Type I of the 4 a on the left. The central line points to the symbol above it in the forgeries. In the genuine stamps, it points in between two values.


'Missing Die' forgery Type II of the 4 a. The letter that looks like a greec 'pi' has both legs straight instead of slightly bent as in the originals.

 

Brighton (Treherne) forgeries:

These forgeries are quite deceptive. They exist of all three values and in many colours (both watercolours and oil colours).


This could possibly be Brighton forgeries? The 4 a Brighton forgery has a "?"-alike letter just to the left of the "3", which these stamps appear to have.


Most likely a Brighton forgery


I've been told this is a Brighton forgery of the 1 a value.

 

Long "R" forgeries:

Forgeries exist where the leg of the 'inverted R' has a very long tail. The central number is like a squarish blob.


This might be the Long R forgery.

 

Die X Forgeries:

Rare forgeries of the 1/2 a and 1 a values.


Die X forgery of the 1/2 a; image taken from https://www.stampboards.com/viewtopic.php?t=13744&start=350 The first one of the central three strokes is too curved. The word 'Jammu' is strangely done at 1 o'clock (see als https://www.kashmirstamps.com/FWater.html)


Similar blur stamps are listed as Die X forgeries in https://www.kashmirstamps.com/FWater.html

 

Forgeries with star cancels and 'snake' forgery:


Highly dubious stamps, the cancel looks like the one that the stamp forger Oneglia sometimes used on his forgeries. Although these look like the Snake Forgery shown below, there are differences (for example double outer circle).


A 'snake-forgery' (so-called due to the snake like pattern in the lower right hand side), it also has a single outer ring. This forgery appears to be the same as the one illustrated in the Illustrated Catalogue of Postage Stamps by John Edward Grey from 1870.

 

Mirror image forgeries of the 4 a (and 'normal' ones of the other values):


Old forgeries of the 1/4 a value: The inverted "R" ornament is almost at the bottom here, instead of it being more to the left. They also have weird cancels. Also a 1 a forgery from the same source.


These 4 a forgeries are printed in reverse (mirror image)! Note the cancels that appear identical to the ones appearing on the previous forgery type

 

Fournier forgeries (mirror image forgeries):


Fournier forgeries, taken from the Fournier Albums in Geneva (thanks to Gerhard Lang for taking photos of them), all in mirror image. So these forgeries must have been produced before 1928 (when the Fournier Albums were assembled). They are listed under Cachemir (Jummo) in his 1914 pricelist and were offered for 1 Franc (1/2 and 1 anna).


A set of forgeries with the same cancel. They are in 'mirror-image'. See also http://www.kashmirstamps.com/FKash.html. There they are shown without cancels, they are reported in the colours blue, red, black, grey and brown.

 

Modern forgeries (1980's):


A whole bunch of forgeries of different states, all made by the same forger, I presume (from Allahabad in India). The Jammu and Kasmir forgery is at the bottom left. I've seen exactly the same forgery in the color green as well. The second item in the bottom row is also supposed to be from Jammu and Kasmir (a Jammu iron-mine seal copied from a Stanley Gibbons catalogue); this seal was temporary used as a stamp (extremely rare), it is usually a 'cancel' in black. Millions of these forgeries were dumped in the market in the 1980's-90's.


Copied from the Stanley Gibbons catalogue 'Stamps of the British Empire 1912', these images were the inspiration for the modern forgeries made in India. Note that the 1 anna and 4 annas values are wrongly attributed.


The same forgery as in the above collection. The circular frame imperfections are always identical for each value. These are most likely modern forgeries printed in the 1980's. They were printed in red, purple, green, black, blue and yellow. I've seen many of these forgeries being offered as genuine. In fact, most stamp offered on Ebay are these (worthless) forgeries. Badly printed so-called Indian forgeries of mainly British Colonies also exists, they are probably from the same source, since both appear with forged "975" cancels.


Sheetlets of these modern forgeries.


Supposed to be from Jammu and Kasmir (a Jammu iron-mine seal copied from a Stanley Gibbons catalogue); this seal was temporary used as a stamp in red (extremely rare), it is usually a 'cancel' in black (but very blur) on the circular Jammu and Kashmir stamps.

 

German(?) Forgeries:


Some other forgeries made by the same forger. They all have a "670" numeral cancel. Forgeries with this "670" cancel also exist for the 'old rectangular' issues. Images of these forgeries appear in an old German stamp preprinted album (see next image).


German stamp album with the above forgeries printed.
