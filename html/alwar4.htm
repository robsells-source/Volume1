The 1/2 a stamps have three more or less parallel lines in the central design. The 1 a has a straigth line with a curved line at the bottom. The 4 a stamp has a single line in the centre. Specialists distinguish between watercolors and oilcolors (the watercolors created problems during the rainy season). These stamps were handstamped and are thus usually very crudely executed. Different kinds of papers were used (native papers are the rarest) and both water based ink (not water resistent!) and oil based ink were used. Stamps used in Srinagar (Kashmir) have red cancels. These stamps were valid up to 1878.

At first I did not understand why every stamp had to be issued in four colors...... D.P.Masson says the first stamps were issued in black and blue both in Jammu and Kashmir. Later Kashmir used the rectangular stamps and Jammu continued to use circular stamps, but now printed in the colors corresponding to those the Kasmir stamps.

There seems to have been some confusion about the 1 a and 4 a values, which were initially attributed to the wrong stamps in earlier catalogues.


Copied from the Stanley Gibbons catalogue 'Stamps of the British Empire 1912'. Note that the 1 anna and 4 annas values are wrongly attributed. The 1 anna should have been 4 annas and the 4 annas should have been 1 anna. Stanley Gibbons apparently only corrected this error in 1985. The confusion becomes complete when it is realized that the (real) 4 annas stamp is actually a reproduction of a Brighton forgery (see Sefi and Mortimer Chapter XVI, Forgeries; the character just to the left of the "3" on top looks like a question mark "?").

I have seen forgeries of these stamps, printed from the original die. They are different from the so-called Brighton forgeries made by Harold Treherne from 1902 to 1907. These forgeries are rather deceptive. See also http://www.sspak.com/library/India/Jammu%20&%20Kashmir%20Forgeries.pdf.
The site http://www.kashmirstamps.ca/SMcontents.html contains valuable information about these stamps. It is an (improved?) online version of the book 'The Stamps of Jammu-Kashmir' by Alexander Séfi & C.H. Mortimer (1937). Publisher: Séfi, Pemberton & Co., Ltd., 12 South Molton Street, W.1. London. 322 pp, 59 black & white plates. The forgeries described are the so-called 'Die I' forgeries (longtime believed to be the genuine stamps!) and 'Missing Die' forgeries.

'Missing Die' forgeries, believed to be from a 'missing die' that could not be found in the archive, it later turned out to be forgeries. These forgeries appear to have been made (and sold) by corrupt post office officials. Click here for these Missing Die forgeries.
